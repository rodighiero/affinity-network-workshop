A hands-on lab for the Text page. Bring the long text you prepared, work through these in order, and treat every number as something to question — not to trust. The last exercises point toward the Network: keywords and phrases are exactly the material a similarity map is built from.

The Text page is a microscope. These exercises teach you to read what it shows — and to notice where it is wrong.

Work from your own text. A 300–800 word excerpt makes cause and effect easier to see than a full paper, so isolate one section if your document is long. Everything assumes English: the readability, sentiment, and entity logic all break on other languages.

1

Read your text as data

MeasurementsVocabulary

TaskAnalyze your text, then describe it in two sentences using only numbers — word count, average sentence length, lexical diversity, reading time.

LearnA document has a measurable shape before it has meaning. This is the vocabulary for everything that follows.

2

Predict, then check

ReadabilitySentiment

TaskBefore clicking Analyze, guess your reading-ease band and your overall tone. Then run it and compare with what you expected.

LearnWhere intuition and measurement diverge — and that “readability” and “tone” are formulas, not judgments.

3

Find the keywords a network would use

Keywords

TaskCompare the most frequent and most distinctive keyword lists. Write down the five words you would choose to represent this text among others.

LearnDistinctiveness, not frequency, gives a text its identity — the same intuition behind the affinity map, which discards the terms everyone shares.

4

Phrases as concepts

Recurring phrases

TaskRead the frequent and strongly bound phrase lists. Do the bound phrases name your text's real concepts better than single words? Pick one and find where it appears.

LearnMeaning lives in multi-word units, and tight collocation is not the same as raw frequency.

5

Break the tool on purpose

EntitiesSentiment

TaskIn Entities, find two mistakes — a stray word tagged as a person, a name split in half, a false organization. In Sentiment, find a word scored positive or negative that is neutral in your context.

LearnAutomated analysis is heuristic. Before you design with these outputs, you have to know where they lie — and decide what to trust.

6

The narrative arc

Paragraph by paragraph

TaskRead the paragraph table as a curve over time. Where does difficulty spike? Where does sentiment dip? Sketch the reading-ease line by hand and compare it to how the text feels to read.

LearnA text has pacing and mood — an argument for visualizing structure rather than summarizing it.

7

Edit and re-measure

ReadabilityParagraph by paragraph

TaskTake your densest paragraph. Rewrite it to raise its reading ease by about 15 points — shorter sentences, fewer long and multi-syllable words. Paste it back and watch the row move.

LearnThe link between writing choices and the metrics. The numbers become something you control, not something mysterious.

8

Two texts, one lens

All sections

TaskAnalyze two texts — yours and a neighbour's, or your introduction and your conclusion. Which sections separate them most: grammar, keywords, sentiment?

LearnComparison is where analysis becomes mapping — the conceptual seed of the whole network exercise.

Teaching notes

  • Sequence. Exercises 3 → 4 → 8 are the on-ramp to the Network: keywords, then phrases, then comparison, then similarity.
  • Timing. As a 45-minute lab: 1–2 as a five-minute warm-up, 3–6 as the core, and 7 or 8 to close depending on time.
  • English only. The syllable, sentiment, and named-entity logic all assume English; results on other languages are unreliable.
  • Nothing is uploaded. Every layer is computed in the browser, so students can use unpublished or sensitive material freely.
